Shot Glass Stand

For my design and manufacturing class, I undertook a project that was required to combine at least two core manufacturing processes to create a unique and meaningful object. As a traveler who collects shot glasses from each city I visit, I decided to design a custom shot glass stand with a globe as the centerpiece to commemorate my travels and highlight the cultural significance of each city’s shot glass. Below, I outline the steps involved in creating this project.

  • Sketches
    • The initial phase involved creating design sketches. I produced several rough, annotated sketches to visualize the overall concept and layout of the shot glass stand. These early sketches provided a foundation for refining the design in subsequent stages.
  • Prototypes
    • Following the sketching phase, I created rough rapid prototypes to better understand the physical representation of the project. I used various materials to represent different components:
      • Base and Globe: Pink foam for both
      • Platform Rods: Pipe cleaners
      • Platform Bases: Cut plastic cups
      • Rubber Feet: Pins and cotton balls
  • CAD Models
    • With the design refined through prototyping, I developed detailed CAD models of the shot glass stand. These models provided a precise representation of the final assembly and guided the manufacturing process.
  • Fabrication
    • Before starting the physical fabrication, I created an operations sequence outlining each step required to manufacture and assemble the parts. The sequence included the following processes that I used to manufacture all the parts:
    • Creating the Base:
      1. Wood Cutting: I began with a 4” x 4” wooden plank. Using a laser cutter, I cut a circular profile to serve as a guide for a cylindrical hole, then used a hand router to cut the hole to a depth of 0.375”.
      2. Drilling: I drilled two through holes for the screws using a drill press and made a counterbore with a depth of 0.2”.
      3. Shaping and Finishing: I cut a 3/4" x 4" x 4" block from the plank using a bandsaw, then sanded the wood and applied Linseed oil to enhance the walnut's color.
  • Creating the Globe:
    1. Mold Preparation: I 3D-printed molds for sand casting the bronze hemispheres. The molds were designed with drafts to facilitate easy removal from the sand.
    2. Casting: Molten bronze was poured into the sand molds to create the hemispheres.
    3. Post Processing and Brazing: The rough cast hemispheres were cleaned using an angle grinder, hand saw, and belt sander. Tapped holes were added using a mill. The hemispheres were then brazed together using an oxygen torch and low-temp bronze.
    4. Finishing: The globe was sanded from 40 grit up to 1500 grit for a mirror finish. Continental patterns were added using 3D-printed profiles and bead blasting to achieve contrasting matte and mirror finishes.
  • Creating the Platform Rods:
      Cutting and Threading:
    1. Aluminum rods were cut into 2.75” lengths, threaded externally (3/8-16), and sanded for a matte finish.
  • Creating the Platforms:
    1. Disc Cutting: Aluminum rods were cut into 0.7” thick discs.
    2. Platform Cutout: A center hole was drilled, followed by creating a larger, shallow hole with a boring bar on the lathe
    3. Threading: Platforms were tapped with 3/8-16 holes to attach to the rods.
  • Final Product Here is the final result of my project, a shot glass stand which is able to hold four different shot glasses.
