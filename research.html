ILIAD Research

ILIAD Website

At Stanford, I am involved with the Intelligent and Interactive Autonomous Systems Group (ILIAD) within Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (SAIL), which focuses on developing algorithms for AI agents to safely and reliably interact with people. This research encompasses both human-robot interaction and human-AI interaction. During my undergraduate research with the lab, I participated in a joint project with the Toyota Research Institute (TRI). The project had two main objectives:

  1. Skill Segmentation in Driving: Train a model to identify and teach driving skills. The model used in this project was developed by my co-researcher for this project Megha Srivastava in this paper, on the data I had collected.
  2. Interaction with Semi-Autonomous Driving Systems: Educating users on how to interact with and work with semi-autonomous driving systems.
  • CARLA Simulation Setup - Parallel Parking Demonstrations:
      Environment Setup: I explored various CARLA maps to identify a suitable location for parallel parking demonstrations. I set up the simulation to include two dummy cars for the parking scenario and created an ego car for controlled demonstrations.
      Data Collection: Data collection was configured to record states and actions at 15 timestamps per second. The recorded states included x, y position, and rotation angle, while the actions recorded were throttle, brake, steer, and gear (forward or reverse). Approximately 75 samples were collected for training the model.
  • CARLA Simulation Setup - Racing Demonstrations:
      Track Setup: A basic map was used as a rudimentary race track for the racing demonstrations. The setup was similar to that of the parallel parking simulation.
      Data Collection: Similar data parameters were used, with about 75 samples collected for the racing scenario.
  • compILE Model Training:
    • I parsed the collected data to prepare it for model training. I had to create trajectory with rewards objects importing the imitation library to use with tensorflow and convert them to .pkl files to be used with the models. Through hyperparameter sweeps, I successfully achieved skill segmentation for the racing example. The results from this segmentation are now being utilized in the ongoing research for this project.
